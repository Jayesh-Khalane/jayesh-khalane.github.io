Is everything a function?

Why some cookies taste better, why optimization appears everywhere, and why experience may simply be the art of recognizing patterns.

For a long time, I carried the desire to write something meaningful, though I never quite knew where to begin. Ideas arrived like restless birds, circling but never landing. Perhaps that is how all thoughts are born: incomplete, uncertain, waiting for another thought to lean against. Then one evening, while staring at nothing in particular, I found myself thinking about cookies, functions, happiness, and the strange machinery of life. It sounded absurd enough to be worth exploring.

So here I am, gathering fragments of thought and stitching them together into something that may never arrive at a conclusion, but perhaps somewhere along the way, it will feel familiar.

Let us begin with a dangerous sentence: everything is a function. Even chaos bends quietly toward patterns. Even randomness leaves fingerprints.

If the word “function” feels distant or mathematical, do not worry. At its heart, a function is simple. You give it something, and it gives you something back. An input. An output. A question and an answer.

Sometimes it looks like f(x) = 2x. Sometimes it looks like f(x) = x². Simple equations with simple behavior. Yet hidden inside them is a profound truth: every function has places where it rises to its highest point and places where it sinks to its lowest. Mountains and trenches carved into mathematics itself. And no matter how complicated the dimensions become, the search remains the same. We are always trying to find what is best, or what is worst.

And perhaps that is why functions matter. Because human beings spend their entire lives trying to maximize something and minimize something else. Money. Happiness. Meaning. Love. Peace. Pain. Regret. Loneliness. Failure.

Imagine, for a moment, a chocolate chip cookie.

To make one, you gather flour, butter, eggs, sugar, vanilla, baking powder, chocolate chips, perhaps a handful of cashews if you are feeling generous. You mix them in certain proportions, in a certain order, under certain conditions. Then you place the tray into the oven and wait.

What comes out may be extraordinary. Or terrible. Or forgettable.

And there it is again: a function.

A good cookie is simply the output of ingredients, measurements, timing, and heat interacting together.

Which raises a strange question. Is it possible to guarantee the perfect cookie every single time? In other words: is it possible to find the exact variables, in the exact amounts, arranged in the exact order, to achieve the output we desire?

Mathematically, the answer is comforting. In theory, optimal points exist. Somewhere within the landscape of every function lies an Everest and a Mariana Trench. A best possible outcome and a worst one. The difficulty is not their existence. The difficulty is finding them.

But life is not lived inside equations. Most of us are simply trying to reach the refrigerator at midnight without stepping on a Lego.

And so another question emerges from the shadows: can life itself be predicted?

The answer is complicated. Yet patterns exist, even in the disorder of human living.

Take happiness, for example. Happiness depends on variables like economic stability, health, relationships, purpose, hobbies, environment, society, and random events that arrive uninvited and rearrange everything.

Some of these lie within our control. Others do not. You can choose your hobbies. You cannot always choose your circumstances. You can work toward better health. You cannot negotiate with every tragedy.

Life, like baking, does not always provide the ingredients you hoped for.

So what do we do? We optimize what we can.

If relationships matter most, we nurture them carefully. If health begins to collapse, we learn that ambition alone cannot save us. Some variables carry enormous weight, while others barely alter the result at all.

A cookie can survive slightly imperfect baking powder. But forget the chocolate chips, and suddenly something essential is missing.

The same logic stretches across almost everything: Health(physical_state, mental_wellbeing, lifestyle), Confidence(past_experiences, fear, perception), DecisionMaking(risk, opportunity, consequences). Different names. Same mathematics.

Everything becomes a question of how well we understand and shape our functions.

But life grows stranger still.

Some functions depend on other functions. Success may depend on happiness, which depends on health, which depends on choices, which depend on experience, which itself depends on time and suffering and memory. Functions nested inside functions endlessly, like mirrors facing each other.

Perhaps there exists a great hidden equation beneath it all: life(success(decisions(...)), happiness, relationships, health, purpose, adaptability, growth...).

A terrifying thought. And yet, somehow, beautiful.

Now comes the part that unsettled me most.

There exists a way to approximately learn the behavior of functions, even when we do not know the exact equation itself.

Machine learning. Neural networks.

If enough input-output examples are collected, patterns begin to reveal themselves. The machine learns. Not perfectly, but closely enough to predict outcomes it has never seen before.

Which leads to an irresistible temptation: could we learn the function of life itself?

Could a machine someday answer questions humanity has carried for centuries? How do I live well? How do I become successful? How do I find meaning?

Perhaps not completely. Life leaks chaos through every crack. A missed conversation alters a future marriage. A delayed train changes a career. A cookie baked on one evening instead of another means missing the room where an opportunity waited.

The butterfly effect is merciless that way.

Yet even without perfect prediction, we already possess something remarkably similar.

Experience.

Consider an old professor who has taught thousands of students over decades. Somewhere along the years, patterns begin to emerge. After only a few conversations, the professor senses who is lost, who is afraid, who is brilliant but undisciplined, and who is silently drowning.

Their advice often changes the direction of an entire life. Not because they possess magic, but because they have seen enough data.

And perhaps that is all wisdom really is: a mind trained by years of observing patterns.

Which raises another troubling thought.

If everything is patterns and data interacting with more patterns and data, then what becomes of creativity? Are our ideas truly original? Or are they merely old fragments rearranged into unfamiliar shapes?

I do not know.

Maybe originality is not the birth of something entirely new. Maybe it is simply the courage to combine old truths differently.

What fascinates me most is this: artificial intelligence requires vast data centers, enormous energy, and machines powerful enough to shake buildings with heat. Meanwhile, the human brain runs quietly on a few chocolate chip cookies, warm milk, and sleep.

But perhaps we should not be arrogant.

Computers, in their modern form, are young. Nature has spent millions of years refining the human mind through evolution.

So the final question remains suspended in the air like smoke: can evolution’s greatest creation eventually be surpassed by its own creation?

Will artificial intelligence one day exceed the human brain in every possible way?

Or will it continue overheating in giant warehouses while we sit quietly in our kitchens, eating chocolate chip cookies and wondering what it means to be alive?
